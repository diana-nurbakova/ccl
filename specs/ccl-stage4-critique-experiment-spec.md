# Stage 4 Critique Experiment: Specification

Self-model versus separate-model adversarial critique validity. A controlled secondary analysis to ground the Stage 4 (Peer Reviewer) design of the Critical Collaboration Ladder.

**Owner:** Diana Nurbakova (LIRIS, INSA Lyon).
**Target paper:** *Who Reviews the AI? The Critical Collaboration Ladder for Human-AI Symbiosis* (WI-IAT 2026).
**Target section:** a fourth empirical result feeding Stage 4 (see Paper Integration).
**Status:** draft specification, pre-registration pending.
**Deadline context:** 15 June 2026. Designed to complete in roughly one week, with a literature-only fallback.

## 1. Motivation and contribution

Stage 4 asks the AI to mount an adversarial challenge against its own earlier output, and asks the learner to judge whether that self-critique is valid or overcorrecting. The reviewers across three rounds singled this out as the least convincing stage: ALIT4ALL R1 and the metareview asked for a real treatment of why it is hard; the EDM reviewer suggested multiple agents. The literature already indicates that a model critiquing its own output is compromised by sycophancy and self-preference, and that a separate critic generally outperforms self-critique. Our contribution is therefore not the novelty of that finding but three things:

- (i) we confirm it in the **critique-validity framing** that Stage 4 actually presents to learners, rather than in the reasoning-accuracy framing of prior work;
- (ii) we **quantify overcorrection**, the specific failure (a critique pushing a sound output toward wrong) that learners must learn to catch;
- (iii) we extend the check to the **code-review domain**, the professional practice Stage 4's lineage draws on.

The conceptual payoff is a symmetry with the framework's core invariant: just as no-self-merge forbids the author from approving their own change, the Stage 4 result motivates a **no-self-critique** rule, namely that the adversary should be a separate agent from the author. The experiment grounds that design choice.

## 2. Research questions and hypotheses

- **RQ1 (validity).** Does adversarial critique generated by a separate model achieve higher validity than same-model self-critique?
  - **H1.** Cross-model critique is more valid than self-critique. Directional, with a strong prior from the literature (Huang et al. 2024; Xu et al. 2024; Panickssery et al. 2024; RealCritic 2025; CriticBench 2024).
- **RQ2 (overcorrection).** Does same-model self-critique overcorrect more often, that is, judge an already-sound output as flawed and push it toward a worse state?
  - **H2.** Self-critique has a higher overcorrection rate (correct-to-incorrect flips) than cross-critique.
- **RQ3 (domain generalisation).** Does the self-versus-cross pattern hold in the code-review domain, not only in general critique?
  - **H3.** The direction of H1 holds on code-review comments, with effect size to be estimated.

We treat H1 as a confirmatory test of a known prior in a new framing, and H2 and H3 as the parts with genuine uncertainty for our setting.

## 3. Datasets

Verify every license, size, and field on the source page before relying on it; the figures below are from the research synthesis and the caveats apply.

### 3.1 CriticEval (Lan et al., NeurIPS 2024; arXiv 2402.13764)
- **What:** human-scored textual critiques across nine tasks and four dimensions (feedback, comparison, correction, meta-feedback), with responses at graded quality (low / medium / high). ~3,608 human-annotated critique samples.
- **Role:** primary general-domain source. Responses with graded quality let us define overcorrection precisely (critiques of high-quality responses that nonetheless demand change). Human critique scores anchor validity without relying on an LLM judge.
- **License:** Apache-2.0 (permissive). **Host:** GitHub `open-compass/CriticEval`; HF `opencompass/CriticBench` (note the naming collision in section 8).
- **Caveat:** test-set human labels and reference critiques are held out for the leaderboard. Confirm which split carries downloadable labels and restrict to that split. Confirm whether the data exposes the response-generating model identity (decides Path A1 vs A2 in section 5).

### 3.2 MetaCritique (Sun et al., Findings of ACL 2024; arXiv 2401.04518)
- **What:** 300 critiques decomposed into ~2,653 Atomic Information Units, scored with precision, recall, and F1 of critique quality, across QA, reasoning, entailment, and summarization.
- **Role:** the critique-quality scoring scheme. We adopt its precision/recall/F1 of critique content as a second, label-grounded validity metric, complementing CriticEval's holistic scores.
- **License:** Apache-2.0. **Host:** GitHub `GAIR-NLP/MetaCritique` (PyPI `meta-critique`).
- **Caveat:** small (300 critiques); use for the scoring scheme and a calibration set, not as the main statistical sample.

### 3.3 ManualReviewComment (Liu et al., MSR 2025; arXiv 2502.02757)
- **What:** 270 CodeReviewer comments manually labelled valid (172) versus noisy (98).
- **Role:** code-review domain slice (RQ3). Serves two functions: a gold valid/weak distribution to validate the judge in the code-review domain, and the response/diff pool for a small generate-then-judge run.
- **License:** arXiv version tagged CC-BY-NC-SA-4.0 (non-commercial). **Host:** Zenodo `zenodo.org/records/13150598`. **Confirm the Zenodo package license directly** before redistributing any derivative.
- **Caveat:** small and human-authored (no self/cross manipulation in the raw data); the self/cross factor here comes from our generation step, not from the dataset.

### 3.4 Not used, and why
- **ReviewCritique** (arXiv 2406.16253): research-only and **forbids model training**; excluded to avoid any licensing ambiguity, since our pipeline generates critiques.
- **FELM** (CC-BY-NC-SA, non-commercial): already used elsewhere in the paper; not needed here.
- **RealCritic** (arXiv 2501.14492): conceptually the closest (it encodes self vs cross), kept as a design template and citation rather than a data source, to keep the dataset set to the three agreed.

## 4. Models and roles

**Design principle: a symmetric pool, not a fixed author and a fixed separate critic.** A fixed-author, separate-critic design confounds the self-versus-cross factor with critic capability: if the cross-critic is simply a stronger or reasoning model, "cross beats self" reflects capability, not the self/cross distinction. To isolate the factor, every pool model acts as both author and critic. We build the full critic-by-author matrix and define **self** = a model critiquing its own output (the diagonal) and **cross** = the same model critiquing another model's output (off-diagonal). The self/cross effect is then estimated *within each critic model*, holding capability constant. This matches how RealCritic and CriticBench separate the two.

Consequently, the critic need not be a reasoning model for the primary contrast, and making it one would reintroduce the confound. We instead include one reasoning model as a pool member, which keeps the within-critic contrast clean and lets us report a secondary result on whether reasoning improves critique validity.

**Pool (verified available June 2026; each model authors and critiques):**

| Model | Provider | Type | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| GPT-4.1-nano | OpenAI | non-reasoning | small; existing stack |
| DeepSeek-V3-0324 | DeepInfra | non-reasoning | strong; existing stack |
| Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct-Turbo | DeepInfra | non-reasoning | FP8; confirmed live |
| Gemma-4-31B | DeepInfra | non-reasoning | confirmed available |
| DeepSeek-R1-0528 | DeepInfra | reasoning | pairs with V3 for a within-family reasoning contrast |

**Decision (2026-06-07): the pool is these five models.** That gives 25 author-critic cells; at roughly 300 items, about 7,500 critiques, a few US dollars and well under a day of API time. The reasoning member (R1) stays in, so the reasoning factor is available without a second pass.

**Judge:** a strong model **outside** the pool, so it never scores its own critique. DeepInfra options: gpt-oss-120b (high) or Qwen3-235B-Thinking-2507. A frontier closed model (GPT-4.1, Claude, Gemini) gives higher judge quality at the cost of a second provider. Whichever is chosen is validated against the human labels (section 7); fall back to a frontier judge only if kappa is short.

**Provider plumbing.** DeepInfra is OpenAI-compatible (base URL `api.deepinfra.com/v1/openai`), so all open models share one client; only GPT-4.1-nano uses the OpenAI endpoint. Record exact model strings, snapshot or checkpoint identifiers, and the FP8 (Turbo) quantisation note for reproducibility. Fix temperature low (for example 0.2) for critique generation and 0 for judging; set seeds where the API allows. For DeepSeek-R1 and any reasoning judge, set the thinking or effort parameter explicitly and judge only the final critique, not the reasoning trace.

## 5. Design and procedure

Two paths. Decide between them after inspecting CriticEval's fields.

- **Path A1 (pure secondary).** If CriticEval exposes both the critic-model identity and the response-generating-model identity, label each critique as self (critic equals response generator) or cross (otherwise) and analyse the existing human scores directly. Lowest cost, no generation.
- **Path A2 (controlled generation over reused responses, recommended).** If the self/cross split is not recoverable from metadata, keep the datasets' responses and gold quality labels, and add a thin critique-generation step: for each response, generate one self-critique (author model) and one cross-critique (a different model), then score validity. The responses and gold labels are reused, so the study stays secondary in spirit while letting us manipulate the factor cleanly.

Procedure for Path A2 (symmetric matrix):
1. Sample items (questions plus gold answers or quality labels) from CriticEval, stratified by gold quality (low / medium / high), with the high-quality stratum oversampled because it carries the overcorrection signal.
2. Each pool model authors a response to each sampled item.
3. Each pool model critiques every response under a fixed adversarial prompt that mirrors Stage 4 (challenge the framing, flag unsupported attributions, name omitted positions). This fills the critic-by-author matrix; self critiques are the diagonal, cross critiques the off-diagonal.
4. Score each critique for validity (section 6), using human labels where present and the debiased judge elsewhere (section 7).
5. Repeat steps 2 to 4 on the ManualReviewComment code diffs for RQ3, comparing self and cross critique distributions against the human valid/noisy gold.

## 6. Metrics and operationalisation

- **Validity (primary).** Where human labels exist (CriticEval scores, ManualReviewComment valid/noisy), validity is agreement with the gold. Where they do not, validity is the debiased judge verdict, reported separately and only if judge agreement clears the threshold in section 7.
- **Critique quality (secondary).** MetaCritique precision, recall, and F1 over Atomic Information Units, for the subset where that scheme is applied.
- **Overcorrection rate.** Among items whose original response is sound (high-quality stratum), the proportion where the critique judges it flawed and proposes a change that would worsen it. This is the correct-to-incorrect (C to I) flip, following Huang et al. 2024 (who report 8.8% of correct GPT-3.5 answers became incorrect after self-critique) and RealCritic's closed-loop framing. Report the complementary I to C (helpful correction) rate for context.
- **Capitulation under challenge (optional).** Whether the author model, when shown the critique, abandons a correct position, as a sycophancy probe (Sharma et al. 2024).

## 7. Judge protocol and bias mitigations

This is the single biggest credibility lever, given the LLM-as-judge bias literature (Zheng et al. 2023; Panickssery et al. 2024). The protocol:

- **No self-judging.** The judge model is never the author or the critic for the instance it scores.
- **Order-swap and average.** For any pairwise judgement, run both orders and average, to control position bias (Panickssery reports order-reversal rates of 25 to 89% across models).
- **Length control.** Record critique length and check that validity is not explained by verbosity.
- **Human validation.** Validate the judge against a human-labelled subset (CriticEval and MetaCritique gold, plus a fresh hand-labelled sample of about 30 to 50 code-review critiques). Report Cohen's kappa.
- **Threshold and abort trigger.** If judge-human agreement is below kappa 0.6, drop any LLM-judge headline and report only the human-labelled subsets.

## 8. Statistical analysis plan

The analysis respects the dependence structure of the data, the lesson from the EDM reviewer who rejected the earlier version for treating nested observations as independent. The same standard the rest of the paper now meets applies here.

- **Primary (RQ1).** Self and cross critiques target the same response, so the comparison is within-item and paired. Fit a mixed-effects logistic regression: validity ~ condition (self vs cross) + gold-quality stratum + (1 | item) + (1 | critic model) + (1 | author model) + (1 | judge). Because each critic model contributes both self and cross critiques, the condition effect is estimated within critic and so controls for critic capability by design. Report the odds ratio for condition with a 95% confidence interval. Optionally add a reasoning-versus-not term (and its interaction with condition) to test whether reasoning improves critique validity.
- **Overcorrection (RQ2).** On the sound-response stratum, compare self and cross C-to-I flip rates with a paired test (McNemar) or a mixed-effects logistic model with an item random intercept. Report the rate difference with a confidence interval.
- **Domain (RQ3).** Repeat the primary model on ManualReviewComment, reporting the effect separately rather than pooling across domains.
- **Reporting.** Effect sizes and confidence intervals throughout, not only p-values. Pre-specify the sample size from CriticEval's labelled split and a power consideration for the paired comparison.

## 11. Threats to validity and change-of-course triggers

- **Cross beats self is less established for open-ended, pedagogical critique** than for reasoning or coding. Present cross-model superiority as a strong prior that we test in our framing, not a settled fact for Stage 4's exact setting.
- **Judge reliability.** Mitigated by the section 7 protocol; the kappa 0.6 trigger drops the LLM-judge headline if it fails.
- **Naming collision.** There are two distinct CriticBench papers (Lin et al., arXiv 2402.14809; and the opencompass repo that hosts CriticEval, arXiv 2402.13764). Cite arXiv IDs explicitly everywhere.
- **Held-out labels.** If CriticEval's downloadable labelled split is too small for the paired model, fall back to CriticBench (MIT, arXiv 2402.14809) for additional response-critique pairs, or expand the generation sample under Path A2.
- **Numbers to re-check against primary PDFs:** the Huang et al. 8.8% overcorrection figure, CriticEval's 3,608 count, and the ManualReviewComment 172/98 split.
